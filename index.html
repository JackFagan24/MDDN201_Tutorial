Intro

Internet infrastructure is a lot like the internet itself; Whilst almost everywhere, it can be hard to notice without a trained eye; this page acts as a short field guide to a few of the physical manifestations of the internet you can experience and witness in Wellington City.

Routers + Modems

A modem connects you to a wide area network (WAN) or the internet. On the other hand, a router connects your devices to your local area network (LAN) or WiFi network, and it lets your devices communicate with each other wirelessly

Example A: The Modem connects to a power source within the home and connects you to the wide area network (WAN) via the Internet Service Provider (ISP).

Example B: A router allows multiple devices to access the internet and to communicate wirelessly by connecting to the Local Area Network (LAN) or the Wifi network.

Cabinets

When walking around Wellington you may notice large cabinets, either painted in the traditional dark green colour often used by Wellington City Council or a lighter lincoln green. The cabinets are roughly 1.5m tall and wide and distribute the fibre and copper internet network from the local exchange to each street in the surrounding neighbourhood via aerial or underground cables.

Example A: Generally, a light beige colour with a manhole on the ground within close proximity; defining characteristics are the rounded right hand side and single cabinet door.

Example B: It is most commonly a dark green colour, but it can often have a commissioned mural on it. They are square in shape and have two cabinet doors.

Plinths

Located just outside the boundary of a property, you will often see dark grey or green plinths roughly 30cm to 60 cm tall. These connect the copper or fibre internet network from a cabinet to the property they are placed on.

Example A: These plinths are located alongside Example B, they are dark grey/black in colour and range from around 30cm to 60cm in height. They often have a number printed on them.

Example B: Approximately 40cm in height, these plinths are generally a light green colour and also feature a number printed on them.

Manholes

Companies such as chorus have small manholes that allow their technicians easy access to the underground network of cables. These can come in a range of sizes and shapes and can be located anywhere from the suburbs to the inner city.

ETP (External Termination Point)

External Termination Points, or ETPS, are small white plastic boxes attached to the outside of a household or property. They are connected to the wider network via the plinths on the property boundaries, either underground or aerially, and then taken through the premise wall and connected to the wiring inside.

Example A: This ETP exhibits plastic tubing that extends out the bottom of the ETP and into the ground, where it is then connected to a nearby plinth

Example B: The notable difference with Example B is the wiring that extends out the bottom is directed to the top of the house, where it connects to a line atop a street post.

Cell Towers

Cell Towers or Base Stations have a multitude of electronic equipment and antennas used in the sending and receiving of signals between mobile devices; They can be a single large monolith or attached to tall structures such as buildings, billboards or monuments.

5G towers (called small cell towers) are often smaller than previous 4G towers and range from 7 to 12 meters high. Small cell 5G towers send and receive high-speed data to cell phones and other devices within short distances

Though Smaller than 4G towers (roughly 7m to 12m in height) they are able to send data faster and more efficiently
